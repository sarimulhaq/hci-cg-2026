User Involvement and Safety in Design

A key lesson in early HCI is that interface decisions directly affect user safety and performance. In safety-critical systems, such as medical devices, even a small input mistake can lead to serious consequences.

Consulting real users helps designers understand how people actually interact with systems. Interfaces built without user feedback may look correct but behave unpredictably during real use.

Iterative Design and Prototyping

Good interface design is rarely perfect on the first attempt. Iterative design encourages repeated testing, feedback, and refinement so problems are discovered early.

Prototypes and mock-ups allow designers to experiment with ideas quickly and safely. This reduces development risk and leads to more usable final systems.

Visual Perception and Context

Human perception is powerful but influenced by surrounding context. Visual illusions demonstrate how the brain interprets shapes based on learned expectations rather than pure measurement.

Designers must be aware that layout, contrast, and visual framing can unintentionally mislead users or create confusion.

Reference: Visual processing & illusions — Pages 19–21

Memory Limits and Information Organization

Human short-term memory can hold only a limited amount of information at once. Interfaces that overwhelm users with unstructured data increase cognitive load.

Grouping information into meaningful chunks supports understanding, recall, and smoother interaction.
